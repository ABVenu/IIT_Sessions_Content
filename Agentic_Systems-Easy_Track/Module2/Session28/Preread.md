## The 2-Minute Context

Every single day, an algorithm is quietly predicting a number that affects your life.

The cab app tells you the fare before you even step inside. The food delivery app shows you a 28-minute estimate the moment you place your order. The bank approves or rejects your loan based on a predicted risk score. The job portal suggests a salary range for a role you're considering.

None of these are guesses. None of them are copy-pasted from a table. A machine looked at thousands of past examples — and learned a formula.

That is exactly what you are about to learn to build.

This session is about **Regression** — the art of teaching a computer to predict a *specific number*, not just a category. By the end of two hours, you will have written Python code that learns from data and predicts answers it has never seen before.

No math degree required. No prior coding expertise assumed. Just curiosity.

---

## The "Secret Sauce"

Here is the single most important idea to carry into this session:

> **Think of it like a taxi meter.**
> A taxi meter starts at a base fare — say ₹30 — the moment you sit down. Then it adds ₹15 for every kilometre you travel. The meter doesn't guess. It uses a simple formula: *Base + (Rate × Distance)*.
>
> Linear Regression does exactly this — except instead of a taxi company setting the base fare and rate manually, the **computer figures them out on its own** by studying thousands of past trips.

That is it. That is the core of everything in this session. The machine reads your historical data, reverse-engineers the formula that fits it best, and then uses that formula to predict future numbers it has never seen.

Simple? Absolutely. Powerful? Enormously.

## Burning Questions — Only Answered in the Live Session

These are the questions that will be resolved during class. Let them sit with you:

1. **The model is just a formula with two numbers — so why can't I just calculate those numbers myself with a calculator?** What is the computer actually doing during "training" that a human can't just do manually in a few minutes?

2. **If a model scores 99% accuracy on its practice data, shouldn't that mean it's incredibly good?** Why might a perfect score on past data actually be a *warning sign* that the model is broken?

3. **When a model makes a wrong prediction, the error is just a number — positive or negative. But what does the *pattern* of all those errors actually tell you?** What hidden truth about your model can you only see when you look at all the mistakes together?
